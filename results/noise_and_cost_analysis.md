# Noise Calibration and the Cost Model in the Knowledge Manifold

This document explores the mathematical calibration of sensing noise to ensure group advantage, and how a robust cost model can be integrated into the knowledge manifold framework to create realistic organizational trade-offs.

## 1. Noise Calibration: Making the Math Work

For the "noisy research signal" (Option C) to drive the simulation, the noise must be calibrated so that solo particles fail but teams succeed. This relies on the statistical property that averaging $N$ independent observations with standard deviation $\sigma$ reduces the standard deviation of the mean to $\sigma / \sqrt{N}$.

### The Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)

Assume the true hidden fitness gradient is a unit vector $g$ (magnitude 1). A particle takes a measurement $m = g + \epsilon$, where $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ in each dimension.
The signal-to-noise ratio for a single observation is $\text{SNR} = 1 / \sigma$.

For a team of $N$ particles averaging their observations, the effective noise becomes $\sigma_{eff} = \sigma / \sqrt{N}$.
The team's signal-to-noise ratio is $\text{SNR}_{team} = \sqrt{N} / \sigma$.

### Required Noise Levels

To make solo particles wander aimlessly, their SNR must be significantly less than 1.
If we set $\sigma = 4.0$, a solo particle has $\text{SNR} = 0.25$. The noise vector is 4 times longer than the true gradient, meaning the measurement is essentially a random direction.

However, a team of 64 particles averaging their observations achieves:
$\sigma_{eff} = 4.0 / \sqrt{64} = 4.0 / 8 = 0.5$
$\text{SNR}_{team} = 1 / 0.5 = 2.0$

A signal-to-noise ratio of 2.0 is highly directional. The team will reliably march uphill.

### The Value of the Researcher Role

This math provides a direct justification for the "Researcher" role.
If a standard "Engineer" has $\sigma = 4.0$, a team of 64 Engineers is required to achieve $\sigma_{eff} = 0.5$.
If a highly skilled "Researcher" has $\sigma = 0.5$, a *single* Researcher provides the same signal clarity as 64 Engineers.

If the team aggregates using a weighted average (where Leaders have high weight and Engineers have standard weight), a team of 5 with one Researcher can achieve high directionality—*if* the team is listening to the Researcher. If a Leader (high weight, $\sigma = 4.0$) dominates the aggregation, the Researcher's clear signal is drowned out by the Leader's loud, confident noise.

## 2. The Cost Model: Economics of Exploration

In the previous simulation, cost was a terrain feature (swamps, cliffs) that penalized movement. In the knowledge manifold model, cost is better framed as the **economic burn rate** of the organization.

### The Three Components of Cost

To make the simulation an economic optimization problem rather than just a search problem, we should implement three distinct costs:

#### A. The Burn Rate (Employee Salaries)
Every particle costs money every time step. This cost is proportional to their capability.
- **Engineers** (standard $\sigma$, high write rate) = Base salary ($1x)
- **Leaders** (high influence weight) = Management salary ($2x)
- **Researchers** (low $\sigma$) = Specialist salary ($3x)
- **Visionaries** (global optimum vector) = Executive salary ($5x)

A team of 64 Engineers costs $64x per step. A team of 5 (1 Leader, 1 Researcher, 3 Engineers) costs $8x per step. The smaller, specialized team is much cheaper but relies on perfect internal dynamics to function.

#### B. The Terrain Difficulty (Exploration Cost)
Instead of penalizing movement, the "Cost Landscape" should represent the **difficulty of learning**.
Some regions of the (pref0, pref1) skill space are harder to research than others (e.g., quantum computing vs. basic web dev).
In a high-difficulty region, the knowledge manifold rises slower per measurement. The team must spend more time (and thus more salary burn) to raise the knowledge manifold to the fitness ceiling.

#### C. The Revenue (Knowledge Exploitation)
How does the organization survive the burn rate? They must generate revenue.
Revenue is generated by teams sitting on high peaks of the knowledge manifold.
- `Revenue = Area under the curve of the Knowledge Manifold * Number of particles exploiting it`
- Alternatively, particles generate revenue proportional to their current Y-height on the knowledge manifold.

### The Organizational Trade-off

This cost model creates a profound tension:
1. **Exploration is pure cost.** A team pushing into unknown territory (where the knowledge manifold is flat) generates near-zero revenue while burning salary.
2. **Exploitation is profitable but finite.** A team sitting on a discovered peak generates high revenue, but if it's a local optimum (a foothill), the revenue might not cover the organization's total burn rate.
3. **The "Valley of Death".** To reach the global summit, a team must leave a profitable foothill, cross a flat/unknown valley (burning cash), and discover the new peak before the organization runs out of money.

## 3. Integrating Cost with Movement

If particles are trying to maximize their own local utility, how does cost affect their movement?

**The Utility Function:**
Instead of just moving in the direction of the noisy fitness gradient, particles move to maximize their expected ROI:
`Utility Gradient = (Expected Fitness Gain) - (Terrain Difficulty Penalty)`

However, particles are short-sighted. They don't want to leave a profitable peak to cross a valley.
This is exactly where the **Visionary** role becomes mathematically necessary. The Visionary feels a pull toward the global optimum that ignores the local valley. If the Visionary has enough influence (or if the Leader listens to them), they can drag the team through the expensive valley of death to find the true summit.

## 4. Summary of the Proposed Mechanics

1. **Noise:** Set base $\sigma \approx 4.0$. Solo particles fail. Teams of ~25+ succeed. Researchers ($\sigma \approx 0.5$) can replace dozens of Engineers in signal value.
2. **Burn Rate:** Particles cost money proportional to their role's power.
3. **Terrain Difficulty:** The old "Cost Landscape" becomes a multiplier that slows down the growth of the knowledge manifold, forcing teams to burn more time/money to learn in difficult domains.
4. **Revenue:** Particles generate value proportional to the height of the knowledge manifold at their current position.
5. **The Goal:** The organization must discover the global summit before cumulative burn rate bankrupts them.
